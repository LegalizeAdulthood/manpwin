Fractals

The word 'fractal' was coined by Benoit Mandelbrot (discoverer of the Mandelbrot set) in 1975 based on the Latin adjective, fractus, and the verb frangere, to break. A fractal is a way of seeing infinity because the basic characteristic of fractals is that they self-repeat as one zooms deeper into them.

Much of the fascination of the fractals comes from the fact that very simple mathematical equations are used to create incredibly complex patterns, a fact which is used in fractal compression.

Fractals appear to mimic many of the 'laws of nature'. Fractals such as IFS (iterated function systems) can be used to synthesise very lifelike trees and ferns using extremely simple equations.

Many fractals come in two forms, those who are defined by their co-ordinates in complex number space (A+iB) and those who form the Julia Set version of these fractals and require a seed point on the complex plane. The appearance of the Julia set is determined by this seed point. Each point on the Mandelbrot set creates a unique Julia Set. This is true for many fractal types. In ManpWIN, try toggling between a fractal and its Julia Set for the point at the centre of the screen by using the space bar.

Many waveforms may be considered as fractals as they theoretically contain an infinite number of harmonics. ManpWIN investigates both the time domain and the frequency domain of waveforms.
